# MOF Synthesis Advisor v0.1 — Chemist's One-Pager

**What this is.** A demo tool that suggests *literature-typical* solvent,
temperature, and synthesis method for a MOF recipe you describe by hand.
Not a replacement for chemical judgment. Built on 4 319 published
syntheses; scaffold-test top-1 ≈ 45 % for solvent, 75 % for method.

---

## How to read the output

| Panel | What it means | What it *doesn't* mean |
|---|---|---|
| **Top-3 solvent clusters** + confidence bars | "Among the literature precedent, these solvent families show up most often for chemistries like yours." | A guarantee that any of these will work. |
| **Temperature recommendation** | Two-stage prediction: first decides RT vs heated, then estimates °C. | Optimal T for *your* product (no yield/purity model). |
| **Time (stub)** | Median `time_h` from training rows in the top-1 cluster. | A real prediction. It's a literature norm. |
| **Synthesis method (top-3)** | 10-class head (solvothermal, room-temperature, slow_evaporation, …). | Reaction kinetics or activation procedure. |
| **k-NN provenance** | The 3 nearest training recipes by Morgan-FP cosine similarity. | Validated peers. They're just the closest data points. |

---

## When to TRUST the recommendation

✔ **Solvent top-1 confidence ≥ 0.7** *and* k-NN top-1 similarity ≥ 0.8
   → the system has strong literature precedent. Use it as a starting point.

✔ **Method top-1 confidence ≥ 0.5** → method head is well-calibrated
   above this threshold (cov@90% = 0.72 on scaffold test).

✔ **Temperature** when method top-1 = `solvothermal` and predicted
   T ∈ [70, 150] °C → this is the densest part of training space.

---

## When to HEDGE

⚠ Solvent top-1 confidence < 0.5 → **use top-3, not top-1.**
   Try the highest-ranked solvent the literature on your specific
   metal also uses.

⚠ All three k-NN similarities < 0.5 → the recipe is far from any
   training point. Predictions are extrapolations; pick the answer
   that best matches your prior chemistry knowledge.

⚠ Method head says `unknown` or `other` in top-1 → the training data
   itself was ambiguous; default to standard solvothermal at the
   predicted T.

---

## When to IGNORE

✗ **OOD-metal warning fires** (yellow banner) → the metal element is
  not in our training distribution. Predictions are guesses.

✗ Predicted temperature > 250 °C or < 25 °C → outside our training
  range support; treat the value as "very hot" / "ambient" only.

✗ All linkers fail to resolve to SMILES (warning shown) → only
  metal/recipe context drove the prediction. Information-poor;
  prefer literature search.

## Three worked examples

### Example 1 — MOF-5 (textbook solvothermal)
**Input.** Metal = `Zn(NO3)2·6H2O`, Linker = `H2BDC`, Modulator = (none),
1 mmol each.
**Output (v0.1 actual).**
- Solvent top-1: `mixed_organic` (top-3 includes `polar_aprotic_amide`)
- Method top-1: `solvothermal` (high confidence)
- Temperature: **93 °C** (literature: ~100 °C in DMF)
**Reading.** ✔ Trust. The model recovers the DMF-family +
solvothermal protocol with T within 10 °C of literature.

### Example 2 — HKUST-1
**Input.** Metal = `Cu(NO3)2·3H2O`, Linker = `H3BTC`, Modulator = (none).
**Output (v0.1 actual).**
- Solvent top-1: `mixed_aqueous`, top-3 includes `polar_aprotic_amide`
- Method: `solvothermal`
- Temperature: **90 °C** (literature: ~85 °C in DMF/EtOH/H₂O)
**Reading.** ✔ Trust top-3 ensemble. The top-1 (`mixed_aqueous`) and
top-2 (`polar_aprotic_amide`) bracket the literature's mixed-solvent
prep correctly. Temperature is spot-on.

### Example 3 — ZIF-8 (ambiguity is honest)
**Input.** Metal = `Zn(NO3)2·6H2O`, Linker = `2-methylimidazole`.
**Output (v0.1 actual).**
- Solvent top-1: `mixed_aqueous` (top-3 includes `methanol`,
  `polar_aprotic_amide`)
- Method: `solvothermal`
- Temperature: **93 °C** (literature spans 25–100 °C)
**Reading.** ⚠ Hedge. ZIF-8 literature is genuinely heterogeneous
(RT methanol-based vs heated DMF-based). The top-3 ensemble *does*
contain methanol (the most common literature solvent for ZIF-8);
prefer methanol + RT if you're aiming for the classic Cravillon/Yaghi
prep. The model leans toward heated solvothermal because the
literature is biased that way.

---

## What this tool will never tell you

- Whether the synthesis will actually *work* in your hands
  (no yield, no purity, no failure prediction).
- Crystal phase, defect density, BET surface area, gas uptake.
- Whether your specific target framework will form vs a competing phase.
- Anything outside the literature it was trained on.

Use it as a **first-pass literature-precedent lookup**, not a synthesis
oracle. Cross-check the k-NN neighbors before committing to a recipe.
